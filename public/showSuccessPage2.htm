Personal StatementGrowing up in Nigeria, I saw how online scams could devastate lives throught marriage scams, cloned social media accounts, fraudulent websites, and SMS messages tricking people into sharing sensitive information like their BVN. Online scams have stolen money, trust, and even lives. My own experience was deeply personal, I lost my entire savings trying to buy a laptop. That money meant so much to me, and losing it left me devastated and depressed. But in that dark moment, I found my purpose. I couldn’t sit back and let others go through the same pain.

In university, I began turning what I have witnessed into purpose. My undergraduate project focused on detecting UDP attacks in software-defined networks using fuzzy logic. It gave me my first taste of how technology could be used to stop threats before they caused harm. That project fueled my curiosity about cybersecurity and set me on a journey to make a real impact.

At BorderlessHr, my professional journey gave me new insights into how AI can transform systems. While I didn’t build the AI models, I was deeply involved in integrating AI endpoints into user interfaces, ensuring that users could interact seamlessly with intelligent systems. It was fulfilling to see how technology could make decisions smarter and processes more efficient, and it reinforced my belief that AI is a powerful tool for solving big problems. At Oaks Intelligence, I was involved in building a system where AI models are used to calculate the transition score and emission score for household buildings.

Outside work, I channeled my passion into community outreach. I’ve organized workshops to educate people about online scams, teaching them how to spot phishing attempts, verify websites, and secure their information. Seeing their confidence grow with knowledge reminded me why this fight matters so much.

While combating online fraud is my immediate focus, my vision doesn’t stop there. I dream of applying AI to healthcare and education,two sectors that hold incredible potential for transformation. I imagine AI systems that can predict disease outbreaks, help doctors make faster and more accurate diagnoses, or personalize learning experiences for students. These innovations could save lives, close educational gaps, and uplift communities.

Every step of my journey, from personal setbacks to professional achievements, has been fueled by a deep desire to make a meaningful impact. What began as a painful experience has evolved into a purpose-driven mission: leveraging AI to build safer, smarter, and more inclusive systems. Through this work, I aim to protect, empower, and uplift not only my community but also the African continents, developing countries, and the world.
Voluntary and Leadership ExperienceLeadership to me is an opportunity to guide, inspire, and empower others to reach their potential while encouraging an environment where people feel valued, heard, and supported.

In 2018, I was appointed vice-president of the Computer Science Department at Federal University, Oye-Ekiti (FUOYE), Nigeria, a role that enabled me to address key academic and career challenges within the community. I observed that many students were struggling academically, and there was a lack of exposure to opportunities in the tech field. I saw an opportunity to tackle these issues by improving academic support and providing clarity for a smooth transition into the tech industry.

To address these challenges, I initiated peer-led tutorials and appointed brilliant volunteers from different academic levels to teach “recalcitrant courses,” thereby creating a learning structure through collaborative efforts. This approach empowered students to take ownership of their learning and resulted in noticeable improvements in grades across the department. Over 300 students benefited from the tutorials, strengthening peer bonding within the department and encouraging students to support each other’s growth.

In terms of career clarity, I spearheaded a tech conference in collaboration with the Google Developers Group (GDG) that included students from Ekiti State University (EKSU). This event provided a platform for students to pitch innovative ideas, with a prize of 100,000 naira for the best concept. I worked closely with external sponsors to secure funding and invited experts from the tech industry to serve as judges. The conference was a major success, attracting over 800 students from my university and not only students from my department, who benefited from expert advice on tech trends, career paths, and job market demands. The event also provided valuable networks and mentors to the participants.

More recently, I demonstrated my ability to influence and inspire young minds through my participation in the Women Techsters Fellowship. As part of this program, I led the "Girls Drive in Tech" initiative, aimed at encouraging secondary school girls to pursue STEM courses. Appointed as the peer leader of a team of five in Ondo State, Nigeria, I organised visits to four secondary schools: St. Louis Girls Grammar School, Fiwasaye Girls Grammar School, CAC Grammar School, and Federal Girls College. Our goal was to challenge the stereotype that STEM is only for boys and inspire the girls to explore opportunities in science and technology.

As the team leader, I spearheaded presentations where we shared our experiences and introduced the girls to successful women in tech to show that it is possible. We fostered discussions to address fears and misconceptions about STEM fields. The initiative impacted the lives of 400+ girls across the four schools, currently enrolled at universities in Nigeria to study STEM courses. It was a fulfilling experience for me as I developed a strong connection with the girls.

These experiences allowed me to develop strong communication and organisational skills which I intend to leverage during my study in the UK by joining societies (such as British Computer Society (BCS)) to contribute positively.
